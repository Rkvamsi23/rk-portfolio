Comedy Rooms As Emotional Labs

stand up practice based research cross cultural humour

From nervous kid to open mics

I was not a natural performer. I used to dread classroom presentations. During graduate school in Taipei I said yes to a dare to try stand up comedy for five minutes. The first set was shaky but it opened a path that now runs through open mics and shows in Taipei, Barcelona, San Francisco and New York, including opening for Netflix comics.

Using sets as experiments

I treat my sets as small experiments where I vary framing, pacing and wording and watch how rooms respond. One recurring example is a story about immigrant frugality.

  • In a club full of people who share that background, it produces a collective sigh of recognition and knowing laughter
  • When the same story is clipped into a short and pushed through recommendation algorithms, people who do not share the context sometimes read it as internalised racism or stereotyping

The emotional function of the bit shifts from healing to offensive as it moves across publics.

What I track

  • Room composition and vibe before the show
  • Where laughter is loud, where it is tight and where it disappears
  • After show comments, especially from people who felt seen or uncomfortable
  • How clips of the same material behave online in comments and engagement

Why this is research for me

Comedy gives me something that lab work cannot: live, embodied feedback from real people who have not come to fill out a survey. Using this practice based lens, I can:

  • see how humour works as a quick way to test boundaries around identity and power
  • study how context collapse on platforms changes the role of a joke
  • generate questions that later feed into more formal analysis and PhD level work

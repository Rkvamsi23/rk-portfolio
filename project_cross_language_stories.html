Feeling a Story Without Language

reception study qualitative coding master's thesis

Questions

I wanted to know whether people can still follow the emotional arc of a story when they do not understand the language, and which elements carry emotion across cultures.

Context

This project grew out of my Global Entrepreneurship Management program. I was already obsessed with films and stand up, and I wanted a structured way to study how narrative feels to someone who is outside the language.

Study design

  • Recruited a small group of participants with different language backgrounds
  • Selected short narrative clips in languages none of them spoke
  • After each clip they wrote:
    • what they thought was happening
    • where they felt tension, relief or a change in the story
  • Treated responses as qualitative data and coded for:
    • descriptions of tension, relief and character change
    • mentions of confusion, surprise or clarity
  • Mapped those codes to the actual narrative beats in the script

Findings

Even when participants could not summarise the full plot, many agreed on the exact moments where something important shifted. They relied on:

  • visual rhythm and pacing
  • framing and body language
  • music cues around key turns

A key outcome was that they often shared an emotional sense of tension and release before they could put the story into words.

My role and methods

  • Framed the research question and designed the experiment
  • Ran all sessions and collected responses
  • Coded and analysed the data using simple qualitative coding and basic descriptive stats
  • Wrote up the work as my thesis on storytelling and marketing

What I took forward

This became my first structured attempt to answer two questions that still guide my work:

  • how people feel a story when meaning is incomplete
  • how that response shifts when language and culture change

Later projects on humour and platforms build directly on this idea that emotion can move ahead of clear semantic understanding.
